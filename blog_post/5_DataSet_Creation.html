From the beginning, we leaned in choice of segmentation model towards UNets, as a UNet-based approach had led to the best result for seagrass bed segmentation reported in literature (reference). Fixed-size inputs are also required for a number of other machine learning strategies that perform segmentation or pixel-level classification. We therefore decided to create a dataset of fixed size images to maximize the number of different machine learning approaches we can try. We chose a standard image size of 256 times 256 pixels - a size that is frequently encountered in literature. Moreover, we wanted to make use of the high spatial resolution of the Sentinel-2 mission, and aimed to download images with a resolution of 10 m x 10 m per pixel. This “high” resolution can complicate Sentinel-2 image download as not all satellite bands have such a high resolution. We therefore decided to download Sentinel-2 images through the GEE (Google Earth Engine) package for python; here, a systematic framework on how to download low resolution bands to a higher resolution is automatically provided. And this does what. Even though there are methods how to reliably interpolate a higher resolution for lower resolution bands (e.g. the Sen2Res plugin for SNAP - DOI:10.1109/TGRS.2017.2694881), we did not use them due to computational and time constraints. Instead, we used the images as provided by the google earth engine for further processing. Out of the 13 bands in the Sentinel-2 images, we downloaded the images with the following 12 bands : B1, B2, B3, B4, B5, B6, B7, B8, B8A, B9, B11, B12. Next, we divided the marine areas of Greece and Croatia into a regular grid of 2560 x 2560 m squares (to obtain images of 256 pixels x 256 pixels with a 10 m resolution). Initially, we thought about more sophisticated methods to define regions of interests. For example, we thought about drawing regular rectangles around a seagrass bed polygon’s center. However, in general pixels of seagrass beds made up a small percentage of total water pixels of any image of 2560 m x 2560 m for both Greece and Croatia. Just dividing these two marine areas by a regular grid yielded the highest number of images where at least 5% of the water pixels would belong to seagrass bed areas. On the flipside, using a regular grid also led to some images having no seagrass beds in them at all. We decided that taking a single image per site could potentially introduce errors. There is the option when downloading Sentinel-2 images through google earth engine to take average images for a given time period. However, we were unsure about which biases this may introduce and if training a machine learning algorithm on a low-variance dataset may reduce the predictive power of the model. Therefore, we settled on taking two images (recorded on different days) per site. During our preliminary research, we had learned that all species of seagrass exhibit seasonal variance in their color (cfr. e.g. [6]). Thus, we anticipated that it would be better to narrow down the time period in which to take a set of duplicate images. Since we could not find information in the literature which time of the year would be best, we decided for two seasons - summer (date range: 2022-06-15 to 2022-08-15) and winter (date range: 2022-12-15 to 2023-02-15) from the image collection COPERNICUS/S2_SR_HARMONIZED. This Sentinel-2 product allows for easy filtering for clouds in order to get cloud free images for further processing. We then used a standardized python protocol to download Sentinel-2 images of interest for the given time periods through the Google Earth Engine API. Download was performed through the accounts of several users involving more than a dozen collaborators. Participants would then use standardized python protocols - mainly based on the python library rasterio - to create masks for land, water, and seagrass directly based on the open-source GT data from Greece and Croatia, as well as land masks based on the OpenStreetMap project (downloaded in February 2024). These resulted in two data sets of raw Sentinel-2 images and corresponding masks - one for winter months and one for summer months. In total, this resulted in 16000 images (approximate) from both the regions.
